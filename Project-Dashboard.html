Measuring Data Exposure in LLM-Integrated Productivity Tools

How much of a confidential document is silently transmitted to a writing-assistant's servers when its browser extension is running by default — even after the user is told not to share the file with AI tools.

How the measurement works

Threat model: a user pastes a received confidential file into a normal text field
1

Plant secrets

A synthetic 2,065-char memo carries 12 unique fictional identifiers, incl. a UUID canary.

2

Paste it

Into a plain field on a local page, with the tool's extension running by default.

3

Intercept

mitmproxy decrypts the HTTPS traffic leaving Firefox on Kali Linux.

4

Measure

A capture add-on scans traffic for the document (20-char windows) & the 12 secrets.

5

Compare

Tool run vs a no-extension baseline, repeated 5× for reproducibility.

Headline metric: how many of the 12 planted secrets reached the servers (the canary makes any match unambiguous). Supporting metric: % of document characters transmitted. Exposure counts outbound traffic only (client → server).

Tool progress

Two independent automatic grammar checkers, plus a no-extension control
Scope. The study measures automatic / background transmission — tools that send the document with no user action beyond pasting. Two independent grammar checkers (Grammarly, LanguageTool) are compared against a no-extension control. On-demand paraphrasers were considered but fall outside this scope: they only transmit text the user actively submits, so they don't pose the same passive-exposure risk.

What each tool says about your data

The vendors’ own stated policies — read July 2026
Grammarly
Keeps it: yes — up to ~1 year; Editor docs until you delete them.
Trains on it: yes, by default (individual accounts; opt-out available).
LanguageTool
Keeps it: no — states it does not store submitted text.
Trains on it: no — does not train on user input.
Self-hostable → text can stay offline.
QuillBot
Keeps it: yes — stores inputs by default (since Nov 2025).
Trains on it: yes, by default (opt-out; Team plan excluded).
ProWritingAid
Keeps it: no — deletes text right after processing.
Trains on it: not stated to train on user text.
Wordtune
Keeps it: cloud processing; uses Google Analytics.
Trains on it: limited public detail — cloud AI rewrite.
Caveat: these are the vendors’ own claims. My measurement proves the text leaves the machine; whether they keep or train on it happens on their servers and cannot be verified from the network.

Related research

Prior work this project builds on — quick reference
Starov & Nikiforakis
WWW 2017
Extended Tracking Powers
First large-scale measurement of extension privacy leakage. 618 of the top 10k extensions leaked browsing/search history — often accidentally via the HTTP Referer header. Method: dynamic analysis + string search.
Relates: the seminal browsing-history line; my work measures document content.
Weissbacher et al.
ACSAC 2017
Ex-Ray
Detects history-leaking extensions even when the leak is encrypted/obfuscated — 184 confirmed of 10,691. Method: network counterfactual analysis + ML.
Relates: shows leaks hide inside encryption — exactly why I decrypt with mitmproxy.
Aggarwal et al.
EuroS&P 2018
I Spy with My Little Eye
Found 218 “spying” extensions across the entire Chrome Web Store (history, tokens, geolocation). Method: an RNN over browser API-call sequences.
Relates: whole-store detection; I go deep on specific tools with proof.
Chen & Kapravelos
CCS 2018
Mystique
Dynamic taint tracking at scale: 181,683 extensions, 3,868 (2.1%) leaking; top leakers >60M users. Method: in-browser taint tracking.
Relates: the heavyweight alternative to my mitmproxy + canary approach.
Nayak et al.
WWW 2024
Sensitive Data Access by Extensions
A malicious proof-of-concept passed Chrome Web Store review; all top-10k login-page passwords are JS-accessible; ~1k sites store passwords in plaintext. Method: PoC + corpus analysis.
Relates: credential-field risk — adjacent to document-content leakage.
Xie et al.
USENIX 2024
Arcanum
3,028 extensions collect sensitive on-page content (up to 144M users); 202 exfiltrate actual page content. Method: dynamic taint tracking on instrumented Chromium.
Relates: closest large-scale ‘content’ study; I quantify a specific document with per-secret proof.
Vekaria et al.
USENIX 2025
Big Help or Big Brother?
Audited GenAI browser assistants: several send the full HTML DOM + form inputs to their servers (Merlin leaked an SSN). Method: MITM TLS decryption.
Relates: the same method as mine, on AI assistants — mine is the writing-assistant, canary-proven complement.
